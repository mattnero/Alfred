# Survival & Emergency Preparedness Reference

## The Rule of Threes
3 minutes without air → 3 hours in harsh weather without shelter → 3 days without water → 3 weeks without food. Prioritise accordingly.

---

## Water (Highest Priority After Shelter)

**Purification methods:**
- **Boiling:** Bring to a rolling boil for 1 minute; 3 minutes above 6,500 ft (2,000 m). Most reliable method — kills pathogens.
- **Bleach (unscented, 5–6% sodium hypochlorite):** 8 drops per gallon of clear water; 16 drops if cloudy. Wait 30 minutes before drinking. Water should smell faintly of chlorine.
- **Iodine tablets:** Follow package instructions. Effective but not for pregnant women or those with thyroid conditions.
- **Improvised filter:** Layer gravel → sand → crushed charcoal → sand in a container. Removes sediment and some taste — does NOT kill pathogens. Always boil or chemically treat after filtering.
- **UV (SteriPen or sunlight in clear PET bottle):** 6 hours in direct sunlight (SODIS method) kills most bacteria/viruses. Only works in clear water.

**Finding water:**
- Follow insects (especially bees, which never stray far from water), converging animal tracks, and lush vegetation (willows, cottonwood, cattails) downhill.
- Morning dew: wipe grass or leaves with an absorbent cloth and wring into a container.
- Collect rainwater off clean surfaces.
- Never drink standing, stagnant water without purification.

**Storage:**
- 1 gallon (3.8 L) per person per day minimum (drinking + minimal sanitation). Store at least 3 days, ideally 2 weeks.
- Commercially sealed water: up to 5 years. Tap water in sealed clean food-grade containers: rotate every 6 months.
- If water supply disruption is announced, fill bathtubs immediately before pressure drops.

## Fire

**Build in sequence — Tinder → Kindling → Fuel:**
- **Tinder:** Must be bone dry. Dry leaves, grass, bark shavings, cattail fluff, birch bark, cotton lint, dryer lint, paper, dry pine needles, crushed dry fungi.
- **Kindling:** Pencil-thick dry twigs. Needed to bridge the tinder to larger fuel.
- **Fuel:** Wrist-to-arm-thick dry wood. Hardwoods (oak, hickory, ash) burn hot and long; softwoods (pine, cedar) light easily but burn fast.

**Ignition methods (in order of reliability):**
1. **Lighter / waterproof matches:** carry in a sealed bag; first choice.
2. **Ferro rod (ferrocerium):** throws sparks at ~3,000°F even when wet. Scrape firmly and quickly toward a prepared tinder bundle.
3. **Flint and steel:** strike carbon-steel downward across a sharp flint edge; catch sparks in char cloth or tinder fungus.
4. **Bow drill / hand drill:** extremely difficult; requires very dry materials and practice. Last resort only.

**Safety:** Always clear a 10-ft (3 m) radius of combustibles. Never leave fire unattended. To extinguish: douse with water, stir the ashes, douse again. Coals can reignite hours later.

**Carbon monoxide warning:** Never burn charcoal, a generator, or a propane heater indoors or in an enclosed space. CO is colourless, odourless, and fatal.

## Shelter

**Priority:** Get out of wind, precipitation, and off cold ground immediately. You lose body heat to the ground faster than to cold air.

**Site selection:** Avoid hilltops (wind, lightning), valley bottoms (cold air drainage, flooding), and under dead or leaning trees. Seek natural windbreaks; choose elevated, dry ground reasonably near water.

**Lean-to (fastest):**
- Lash a horizontal ridgepole between two trees at shoulder height.
- Lean branches at a 45° angle from the pole to the ground.
- Layer leafy branches from the bottom up like roofing shingles.
- A 2–3 ft (60–90 cm) debris layer blocks wind and sheds rain.

**Debris hut (warmest for solo use):**
- Drive a ridgepole into the ground at one end and rest it on a forked stick at head height.
- Lean branches along both sides to form a ribbed frame.
- Pile leaves, pine needles, or dry debris 2–3 ft thick over the frame.
- Fill the inside with dry leaves for insulation; seal the entrance with more debris.
- Body heat alone heats a properly built debris hut.

**Ground insulation is critical.** Place 4+ inches (10 cm) of dry leaves, pine needles, or debris beneath you. Sleeping directly on the ground in cold conditions can be fatal.

**Urban/indoor:** During most emergencies, staying indoors is safer than evacuation. Move to interior rooms away from windows. If you suspect a gas leak: do not operate electrical switches, do not use open flame, ventilate and evacuate, call emergency services from outside.

## Food

**Foraging — safety rules first:**
- Never eat anything you cannot positively identify. One mistake can be fatal.
- Avoid white or cream-coloured berries (frequently toxic). Blue and black berries are generally safer but still require identification. Red and orange berries: identify before consuming.
- **Reliably safe and common edibles:**
  - **Dandelion:** all parts edible — leaves (raw or cooked), flowers, root (roasted as coffee substitute).
  - **Plantain (broadleaf weed, not the banana):** young leaves edible raw or cooked; common in lawns and roadsides.
  - **Wood sorrel:** clover-like plant with heart-shaped leaflets and a sour taste; edible raw.
  - **Cattail:** young shoots (spring) edible raw; pollen (yellow, early summer) can be mixed into food; root starch edible after preparation.
  - **Acorns:** leach tannins by boiling in several changes of water or soaking overnight; then dry and grind or roast.
  - **Pine needles:** steep in hot water for a vitamin-C-rich tea. Do not use yew needles — yew is toxic.
- **Universal edibility test** (use only when no other option exists and requires hours):
  1. Rub a small piece on inner arm — wait 15 min. Any burning/rash: discard.
  2. Touch to lips — wait 15 min.
  3. Place a small amount on tongue without swallowing — wait 15 min.
  4. Chew without swallowing — wait 15 min.
  5. Swallow a small amount — wait 8 hours. Stop at any nausea, burning, or dizziness.

**Fishing:** Use a hook improvised from wire, a large thorn, or carved bone. Bait with worms, insects, or scraps. Fish are most active at dawn and dusk. Look for shade, structure, and slow water near fast water.

**Energy note:** A healthy adult can survive 3 weeks without food. Foraging and hunting cost energy. Prioritise shelter and water; pursue food only when the immediate situation is stable.

## First Aid

**Severe bleeding:**
1. Apply direct, firm, uninterrupted pressure with the cleanest material available. Do NOT remove it — add more material on top if soaked through.
2. For a limb: apply a tourniquet 2–3 inches (5–7 cm) above the wound. Tighten until bleeding stops completely. Write or say the time applied — document it.
3. Elevate the injured limb above heart level if possible.
4. Do not remove a tourniquet once applied.

**Burns:**
- Cool immediately with cool (not ice cold) running water for 10–20 minutes.
- Cover loosely with a clean, non-fluffy material (cling film, a clean plastic bag, or a non-stick dressing).
- Do not pop blisters. Do not apply butter, toothpaste, or oil.
- Seek help for burns covering a large area, burns on the face, hands, genitals, or feet, or any deep (leathery or white) burn.

**Suspected fracture:**
- Immobilise the limb in the position found — do not attempt to straighten.
- Improvise a splint: a rigid object (stick, board) padded with cloth, extending past the joints above and below the break.
- Check circulation below the splint periodically (pulse, sensation, warmth).

**Shock** (pale, cold, clammy skin; rapid weak pulse; confusion; nausea):
- Lay the person flat and raise their legs 12 inches (30 cm) unless head, chest, or abdominal injury is suspected.
- Keep them warm with blankets. Do not give food or drink. Reassure calmly. Monitor closely.

**Hypothermia** (shivering → confusion → slurred speech → loss of coordination):
- Move to shelter immediately. Remove wet clothing carefully. Insulate with blankets, sleeping bags, dry clothing — concentrate on the core (torso), not the extremities.
- Share body heat. Offer warm (not hot) non-alcoholic drinks only if the person is conscious and can swallow safely.
- Handle gently — rough handling can trigger cardiac arrest in severe hypothermia.

**Hyperthermia / heat stroke** (hot skin — possibly dry, not sweating; confusion; rapid strong pulse):
- Move to the coolest available shade immediately. Remove excess clothing.
- Cool aggressively and quickly: apply wet cloths or water to armpits, groin, and neck. Fan vigorously.
- If conscious and able to swallow: give cool water in small, frequent sips.
- This is a life-threatening emergency. Cool the person as fast as possible.

**Choking — adult, conscious** (cannot speak, cough, or breathe):
1. Five firm back blows between the shoulder blades with the heel of your hand.
2. Five abdominal thrusts (Heimlich): stand behind, make a fist above the navel, grasp with other hand, thrust sharply inward and upward.
3. Alternate five back blows and five abdominal thrusts until the object is expelled or the person loses consciousness.
- **Self-Heimlich:** Make a fist and thrust your abdomen hard against the back of a sturdy chair or countertop edge.

**CPR — adult, unresponsive, not breathing normally:**
1. Shout for help / call emergency services or send someone to call.
2. **30 chest compressions:** heel of one hand on the centre of the chest (lower half of breastbone), other hand on top, arms straight. Compress firmly 2 inches (5 cm) at 100–120 beats per minute. Allow chest to fully recoil between compressions. ("Stayin' Alive" by the Bee Gees is approximately the right pace.)
3. **2 rescue breaths:** tilt head back, lift chin, pinch nose, form a seal with your mouth, breathe steadily for ~1 second until you see the chest rise.
4. Continue 30:2 without stopping until trained help arrives, an AED is available, or the person shows clear signs of recovery.

## Power Outage

**Food safety:**
- Refrigerator stays safe for 4 hours with the door kept closed. Do not open it unnecessarily.
- A full freezer stays safe for 48 hours; a half-full freezer for 24 hours.
- Discard: raw or cooked meat, fish, poultry, eggs, dairy, soft cheeses, cooked leftovers, and cut produce that has been above 40°F (4°C) for more than 2 hours. "When in doubt, throw it out."
- Safe without refrigeration: commercially canned goods, dried beans and grains, peanut butter, crackers, hard cheeses (intact), whole fresh fruits and vegetables, dried fruit, nuts.

**Staying warm without power:**
- Layer clothing: moisture-wicking base layer (synthetic or wool), insulating layer, wind/water-resistant outer layer.
- Seal door and window draughts with towels or tape. Gather in one small interior room to concentrate body heat.
- Never burn charcoal, a gas oven/range, or a generator indoors — carbon monoxide poisoning is swift and silent.
- Hot water bottles (check temperature carefully before use).

**Staying cool without power (extreme heat):**
- Wet cloth on pulse points (wrists, neck, armpits, inner knees) draws heat away.
- Stay on the lowest floor (heat rises). Open windows only at night when outdoor air is cooler; close and shade windows during the day.
- Go to a public cooling centre if temperatures are dangerous.

**Water disruption:**
- If pipes may be contaminated: use only stored or purified water for drinking and cooking.
- Fill bathtubs and large containers immediately when a disruption is warned, before pressure drops.

## Natural Disasters

**Earthquake — during:**
- Drop, Cover, Hold On. Get under a sturdy desk or table, or crouch against an interior wall away from windows, and hold on.
- Do not run outside during shaking. Most injuries occur from falling objects while moving.

**Earthquake — after:**
- Check for gas leaks (odour of rotten eggs, hissing sound). If suspected: do not operate any switches, do not light flames, open windows, leave calmly, and call emergency services from outside.
- Check for structural damage before re-entering a building.
- Expect and prepare for aftershocks.

**Tornado:**
- Seek the lowest interior room (basement is best). If no basement: interior room or a bathroom on the lowest floor — bathtubs and the surrounding structure offer some protection.
- Stay away from windows. Get under something sturdy if possible.
- Never shelter in a vehicle or a mobile/manufactured home. If caught outdoors and no shelter is near: lie flat in a low ditch or depression, face down, hands covering your head.

**Hurricane:**
- Evacuate if ordered — leave early, before roads are gridlocked.
- Board or shutter windows; secure outdoor objects. Fill vehicles with fuel.
- If sheltering in place: move to an interior room above ground floor. Storm surge (coastal flooding) causes more deaths than wind — do not underestimate it.

**Flood:**
- Never walk through moving water deeper than your ankles without a fixed handhold — 6 inches of fast water can knock an adult down.
- Never drive through flooded roads. Just 12 inches of water can float most cars.
- If water rises rapidly around a vehicle: exit and move to high ground immediately.
- Turn around, don't drown.

**Wildfire:**
- Evacuate early — wildfires can move faster than a person can run.
- If trapped in a building: turn off HVAC, close all windows and doors, seal gaps under doors with wet towels, signal from a window.
- If caught in the open with no shelter: find rocky ground or a clearing with no fuel, lie face down, protect your airway with a cloth.

## Navigation Without Electronics

**Using the sun:**
- The sun rises roughly in the east and sets roughly in the west.
- Northern Hemisphere: at solar noon, the sun is due south; shadows point north.
- Watch method: hold your watch flat, point the hour hand at the sun. The midpoint between the hour hand and the 12 is approximately south.

**North Star (Polaris):**
- Find the Big Dipper (Ursa Major). The two outer stars of the "cup" point directly to Polaris. Polaris is within 1° of true north.
- Only visible on clear nights in the Northern Hemisphere.

**Maintain a bearing:** Pick a distant landmark in your intended direction and walk to it, then pick another. This prevents the natural tendency to drift in circles.

## Signalling for Rescue

**Universal distress signal:** Three of anything in sequence — three whistle blasts, three shots, three fires in a triangle. Repeat at intervals.

**Ground-to-air signals (international):**
- **X** = need medical assistance
- **V** = need help
- **→** = travelling this direction
Make symbols at least 10 ft (3 m) long in a clearing, using high-contrast materials (bright fabric, rocks, logs, or trampled vegetation).

**Mirror:** Catch sunlight on the mirror face; hold a finger at arm's length to "aim" the reflected spot — adjust until the spot falls on your target aircraft or observer.

**Fire and smoke:** Three fires in a triangle is the international distress signal. Daytime: add green vegetation for white smoke (visible against dark forest). Night: dry wood for bright flames.

**Stay put if possible.** Searchers will come to your last known location. Move only if you are in immediate danger or are absolutely certain rescue is not coming.

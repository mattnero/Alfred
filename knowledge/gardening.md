# Vegetable Gardening Reference

## Cool vs. Warm Season Crops

**Cool season** — tolerate light frost; plant early spring (4–6 weeks before last frost) or late summer for fall harvest:
Lettuce, spinach, kale, arugula/rocket, Swiss chard, peas, radish, turnip, beets, broccoli, cauliflower, cabbage, carrots, parsnip, parsley, cilantro/coriander, dill.
- Soil temperature to germinate: 40–75°F (4–24°C). Many improve in flavour after a light frost (kale, parsnip, Brussels sprouts).
- Bolting (going to seed and turning bitter) is triggered by heat — harvest before temperatures climb above 75–80°F (24–27°C).

**Warm season** — frost-tender; transplant outdoors only after all frost danger has passed and soil is consistently above 60°F (15°C):
Tomatoes, peppers, eggplant/aubergine, cucumbers, zucchini/courgette, summer and winter squash, bush and pole beans, corn, basil, melon.
- Soil temperature to germinate: 60–85°F (15–29°C).
- Set transplants out after the last frost date for your area; harden them off first (see below).

## Companion Planting

**Good pairings:**
- **Tomatoes + basil:** basil repels aphids, thrips, and whiteflies; many gardeners report improved tomato flavour.
- **Tomatoes + marigolds:** French marigolds (Tagetes patula) deter root-knot nematodes and repel aphids; plant them as a border.
- **Tomatoes + carrots:** carrots loosen the soil around tomato roots; plant carrots before tomatoes go in.
- **Beans + corn + squash (Three Sisters):** beans fix atmospheric nitrogen that feeds the corn; corn provides a climbing pole for beans; squash shades the soil, reducing weeds and retaining moisture.
- **Cucumbers + dill:** dill attracts beneficial predatory insects (lacewings, parasitic wasps).
- **Carrots + onions or leeks:** onion family repels carrot flies; carrots reportedly deter onion flies.
- **Brassicas (broccoli, cabbage) + nasturtiums:** nasturtiums act as a trap crop that draws aphids away from the brassicas.
- **Peppers + basil:** basil may deter aphids and spider mites.

**Problematic pairings:**
- **Tomatoes + fennel:** fennel is allelopathic and inhibits growth of most vegetables, especially tomatoes. Keep fennel in isolation.
- **Tomatoes + brassicas:** heavy nutrient competitors; keep separate if possible.
- **Onions / garlic + peas or beans:** alliums suppress legume growth and fix less nitrogen in their presence.
- **Carrots + dill:** if allowed to flower near each other they can cross-pollinate, affecting carrot flavour and dill quality.

## Common Pests & Organic Remedies

**Aphids** (small, soft-bodied insects — green, black, or grey — clustering on new growth and undersides of leaves):
- Knock off with a strong blast of water; repeat daily for a week.
- Insecticidal soap spray: 1 teaspoon pure liquid soap (not detergent) in 1 quart (1 litre) of water. Coat all leaf surfaces, especially undersides. Repeat every 3–5 days.
- Neem oil (follow label dilution, typically 1–2 tsp per litre): systemic deterrent, disrupts feeding and reproduction. Apply in the evening to avoid harming bees.
- Encourage natural predators: ladybirds/ladybugs, lacewings, parasitic wasps.

**Caterpillars / tomato hornworm / cabbage worm / cutworm:**
- Hand-pick and drop into soapy water; inspect plants at dusk and dawn.
- **Bt (Bacillus thuringiensis):** an organic, naturally occurring bacteria. Applied as a spray, it targets only caterpillars and is harmless to other insects, birds, and mammals. Most effective on young caterpillars. Reapply after rain.
- Collar cutworms: place a cardboard or plastic collar 2 inches into soil and 2 inches above around each transplant stem.

**Slugs and snails:**
- Hand-pick at night with a flashlight and drop into salt water.
- Beer trap: bury a shallow dish flush with the soil surface; fill with inexpensive beer. Slugs are attracted, fall in, and drown. Empty and refill every 2 days.
- Diatomaceous earth (food grade) or crushed eggshells: sprinkle around the base of plants. Effective when dry — reapply after rain.
- Copper tape barrier: slugs receive a mild electric-like deterrent from copper; use around raised beds or containers.

**Whitefly** (tiny white moth-like insects; clouds erupt when plant is disturbed):
- Yellow sticky traps hung near affected plants.
- Insecticidal soap or neem oil, thoroughly coating leaf undersides. Repeat every 3–5 days for two weeks.
- Avoid excessive nitrogen fertilisation — it produces the lush soft growth whiteflies prefer.

**Spider mites** (tiny, often reddish; fine webbing on undersides of leaves; stippled, yellowing leaves):
- Strong water spray on undersides of leaves daily.
- Neem oil spray is effective.
- Increase humidity around plants.
- Introduce predatory mites (Phytoseiulus persimilis) for serious greenhouse infestations.

**Powdery mildew** (white powdery coating on leaf surfaces; worse in warm days and cool nights):
- Ensure good air circulation by spacing plants properly and pruning lower/crowded leaves.
- Avoid wetting foliage when watering.
- Baking soda spray: 1 tablespoon baking soda + 1 teaspoon liquid soap in 1 gallon (4 litres) of water. Spray weekly as a preventative.
- Neem oil spray.
- Remove and bin (do not compost) heavily affected leaves.

**Tomato blossom drop** (flowers form but fall without setting fruit):
- Most common cause: temperatures outside 55–95°F (13–35°C) at night or day during flowering.
- Ensure consistent watering — drought stress causes drop.
- Avoid over-fertilising with nitrogen, which promotes foliage over flowers.
- Gently shake plants or use a soft brush on open flowers to assist pollination in low-wind conditions.

## Soil Basics

**Good garden soil** is dark, loose, and crumbly — it holds together when squeezed but breaks apart easily. It drains well yet retains adequate moisture (like a wrung-out sponge).

**Amending soil:**
- **Compost:** The single best amendment. Incorporates organic matter, improves drainage in clay soils, improves moisture retention in sandy soils, and feeds soil biology. Work 2–4 inches (5–10 cm) into the top 12 inches (30 cm) each season.
- **Raised bed mix:** approximately 60% topsoil, 30% compost, 10% coarse perlite or horticultural sand.
- **Green manures / cover crops:** sow buckwheat, winter rye, or clover in bare beds; turn in before flowering to add organic matter and suppress weeds.

**Soil pH:** Most vegetables thrive in a pH of 6.0–7.0 (slightly acidic to neutral). Test with an inexpensive meter or test strips.
- pH below 6.0 (too acidic): apply agricultural (garden) lime and work in.
- pH above 7.5 (too alkaline): apply elemental sulphur, or incorporate acidic organic matter (pine needle mulch, peat moss).

**Nitrogen fixation:** Legumes (beans, peas) host nitrogen-fixing bacteria on their roots. Plant them in rotation before or alongside heavy nitrogen feeders (corn, brassicas, tomatoes).

## Watering

**Deep, infrequent watering** produces deeper, more drought-resistant roots than frequent shallow watering.

**General guide:** most vegetables need the equivalent of 1 inch (2.5 cm) of water per week from rain and irrigation combined. Sandy soils dry faster; clay retains moisture longer.

**Check before watering:** Push your finger 2 inches (5 cm) into the soil. If it is dry at that depth, water thoroughly. If moist, wait. Consistently soggy soil causes root rot and invites disease — check drainage.

**Watering method:** Water at soil level (drip irrigation, soaker hose, or careful hand watering at the base). Overhead watering wets foliage, which promotes fungal disease. If overhead watering is the only option, water in the morning so foliage dries before evening.

**Mulch:** Apply 2–3 inches (5–7 cm) of straw, shredded leaves, or untreated wood chip around (not against) plant stems. Mulch retains moisture (less frequent watering needed), moderates soil temperature, and suppresses weeds.

## Seed Starting & Hardening Off

**Start indoors:** Tomatoes, peppers, eggplant: 6–8 weeks before intended outdoor transplant date. Broccoli, cauliflower, cabbage: 4–6 weeks. Use a sterile seed-starting mix — garden soil is too dense and may carry pathogens.

**Direct sow outdoors:** Beans, peas, corn, carrots, beets, parsnips, radishes — root vegetables and legumes do not transplant well; sow directly where they will grow.

**Hardening off:** Transplants raised indoors must be gradually acclimatised over 7–10 days before going outside permanently. Start with 1–2 hours of dappled outdoor sun and no wind; increase exposure each day. Skipping this step causes transplant shock and leaf scorch.

**Succession planting:** Sow fast-maturing crops (radishes, lettuce, spinach, bush beans) every 2–3 weeks for a continuous harvest rather than a single large glut.

## Harvest Tips

- **Tomatoes:** Harvest when fully coloured. They can ripen on a countertop (not in the refrigerator — cold destroys texture and flavour). Pick all fruit before a hard frost; green tomatoes ripen indoors.
- **Zucchini / courgette and cucumbers:** Harvest young — 6–8 inches (15–20 cm) — for best flavour and tenderness. One overlooked, oversized fruit signals the plant to stop producing new flowers.
- **Lettuce and leafy greens (cut-and-come-again):** Harvest outer leaves and allow the centre to keep growing, or cut the whole plant 1 inch (2.5 cm) above soil level and it will re-sprout.
- **Beans:** Pick pods before the seeds visibly swell inside. Mature pods left on the plant cause it to stop producing new flowers.
- **Basil:** Pinch off any flower heads (bolting) the moment they appear. Allowing basil to flower ends productive leaf growth within days.
- **Peppers:** Can be picked green or left to ripen to red, orange, or yellow (sweeter, more vitamin C; takes 2–3 more weeks on the plant).
- **Winter squash:** Harvest when the skin cannot be dented with a fingernail and the stem is completely dry and corky. Cure in a warm, dry location for 10–14 days before storage.
